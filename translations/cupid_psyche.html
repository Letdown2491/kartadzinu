La Rebelatsione / The Revelation

Metamorphoses V.22-24 — Apuleius of Madauros, c. 170 AD

From North Africa's greatest Latin novelist

Introduction

Apuleius (c. 124-170 AD) was born in Madauros (modern M'Daourouch, Algeria), a Berber from the same region that would later produce Augustine. His Metamorphoses (also called The Golden Ass) is the only complete Latin novel to survive from antiquity.

The tale of Cupid and Psyche, embedded within the novel, is one of the most influential stories in Western literature. Psyche ("Soul") is a mortal princess so beautiful that Venus grows jealous. Venus sends her son Cupid ("Desire") to make Psyche fall in love with a monster, but Cupid himself falls in love with her. He visits her only at night, forbidding her to see his face.

This passage is the famous revelation scene: persuaded by her jealous sisters that her husband is a serpent who will devour her, Psyche lights a lamp while he sleeps—and discovers that her "monster" is the most beautiful of all the gods.

This text is uniquely appropriate for Kartadzinu: Apuleius was North African, writing in the ornate "African Style" of Latin prose. His language influenced all subsequent African Latin writers.

This translation showcases:

  • Descriptive vocabulary: Colors, textures, divine beauty
  • VSO narrative: The dramatic revelation sequence
  • Construct state: For body parts and intimate possession
  • Perfective aspect: Completed narrative actions
  • Betacism: The b/v merger (bidai from vidit)

The Text / Lu Tekstu

English

But when the light revealed the bed's secrets,
she saw the gentlest and sweetest of all creatures—
Cupid himself, the beautiful god, lying beautifully in sleep.

At the sight, even the flame of the lamp
brightened with joy,
and the razor regretted its sacrilegious edge.

But Psyche, overwhelmed by such a vision,
grew pale and trembling,
and sinking to her knees
sought to hide the blade in her own breast.

She gazed upon the golden hair of his divine head,
drenched with ambrosia,
the locks straying over his rosy cheeks
and milk-white neck,
some hanging in front, some behind,
the splendor of which dimmed the lamp's own light.

On the shoulders of the winged god
dewy feathers gleamed with white brilliance,
and though his wings were at rest,
the tender down at their edges
trembled and rippled in ceaseless play.

The rest of his body was smooth and gleaming,
such that Venus could feel no regret
for having borne such a child.

At the foot of the bed lay his bow,
his quiver, and his arrows—
the gracious weapons of the great god.

Kartadzinu

Ma kandu la luts rebelau lus sekretas du letu,
bidai la plu mite e la plu dulse de totas las kreaturas—
Kupidu ipse, lu deu belu, dormientu belaménte.

A ista bista, anke la flamma de la lutserna
se klarifikau kon goia,
e la rasora se penitau de sea akja sakrilega.

Ma Psike, sobraprendata de tala bisione,
debenau palida e tremante,
e kajendu a sus genukulus
kerkau kondere la lama en petu-seu propriu.

Kontemplau lus kapilus aureus de kapu-seu dibinu,
banjatus en ambrosia,
las lokkas errantes supra gennas-seas roseas
e kollu-seu blanku kom lakta,
algunas pendentes abante, algunas deretru,
lo splendoru de las kwalas oskurau la luts de la lutserna ipsa.

Supra lus humerus du deu alatu
splendiban plumas roriferus kon blanka klaritate,
e anke si alas-seas reposaban,
lus pluminus tenerus a marginas-sus
tremolaban e undulaban en goku perpetuu.

Lu restu de korpu-seu era lisu e splendente,
talu ke Venus non podeba sentire regrettu
de avere portatu talu filu.

A lu pe du letu djatseba arku-seu,
faretra-sea, e sagittas-seas—
las gratiosas armas du grandu deu.

The Arrow and the Oil

English

While Psyche, in endless wonder,
examined and admired her husband's weapons,
she drew one arrow from the quiver
and tested its point against her thumb.

But pressing too hard with trembling hand,
she pricked herself deeply,
so that tiny drops of rosy blood
appeared upon her skin.

Thus, unknowing, Psyche
fell into love with Love himself.

Then, burning ever more with desire for Desire,
she leaned over him passionately,
showering him with hurried, open-mouthed kisses—
fearing only that he might wake.

But while she lay wounded and swooning
in such sweetness of heart,
the lamp—whether in wicked treachery
or jealous spite,
or because it too wished to touch such a body—
spat a drop of burning oil
onto the right shoulder of the god.

The god, burned, leapt up,
and seeing his trust betrayed and defiled,
flew away in silence
from the eyes and hands of his wretched wife.

Kartadzinu

Mentre ke Psike, en sensa-fine merabilia,
eksaminaba e admiraba las armas de maritu-seu,
trasai una sagitta de la faretra
e probau punta-sea kontra politse-seu.

Ma prementu trop forte kon manu tremulante,
se puntsau profundamente,
tal ke pikulas gottas de sangu roseu
aparitseru supra pele-sea.

Sik, non sapentu, Psike
kajai en amor kon Amoru ipse.

Alora, ardentu sempre plu kon desideriu por Desideriu,
se inklinau supra-elu passionatamente,
kubrientu-lu de basjas rapidas, aberta-bukka—
tementu solu ke elu se ebigilesse.

Ma mentre ke ela djatseba bulnerata e languente
en tala dultsitate de koru,
la lutserna—sibe per malitsia perfida
sibe per inbidia gelosa,
sibe porke ela anke boleba tatsare talu korpu—
espuau una gotta de oleu ardente
supra humeru destru du deu.

Lu deu, ustulatu, saltau,
e bidendu fide-sea traita e biolata,
bolau en silentsiu
de lus okulus e manus de sea misera sposa.

Grammatical Features

Construct State for Body Parts

Body parts, as inalienable possessions, take the construct state throughout:

  • kapu-seu dibinu "his divine head"
  • gennas-seas roseas "her rosy cheeks"
  • kollu-seu blanku "his white neck"
  • petu-seu propriu "her own breast"
  • alas-seas "his wings"
  • korpu-seu "his body"
  • humeru destru "right shoulder"

Betacism

The characteristic African Latin b/v merger appears throughout:

  • bidai < Latin vidit "she saw"
  • bisione < Latin visio "vision"
  • boleba < Latin volebat "it wished"
  • bolau < Latin volavit "he flew"
  • biolata < Latin violata "violated"

VSO Narrative

The elevated narrative register uses VSO word order for dramatic effect:

  • Bidai la plu mite... "She saw the gentlest..."
  • Kontemplau lus kapilus aureus... "She gazed upon the golden hair..."
  • Splendiban plumas roriferus... "Gleamed dewy feathers..."

Descriptive Participles

Latin participles are used extensively as descriptive adjectives:

  • dormientu "sleeping" (present active)
  • banjatus "drenched" (perfect passive)
  • pendentes "hanging" (present active)
  • tremolantes "trembling" (present active)
  • ustulatu "burned" (perfect passive)

Historical Note

Apuleius wrote in the elaborate "African Style" (stilus Africanus) known for its ornate vocabulary, complex syntax, and love of rare words. This passage showcases his descriptive power—the scene has inspired countless paintings, sculptures, and retellings from antiquity to the present. The story of Cupid and Psyche is an allegory of the soul's journey to union with divine love, a theme that resonated deeply with later North African Christians like Augustine.

The African Connection

Apuleius was a Berber who studied in Carthage and Athens before settling in Roman Africa. His Metamorphoses was written in the same cultural milieu that would produce African Romance. The ornate prose style he pioneered became characteristic of African Latin literature for centuries, influencing writers from Tertullian to Augustine.
