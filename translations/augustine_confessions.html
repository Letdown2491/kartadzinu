Sero Te Amavi / Tard Te Amai

Confessions X.27 — Augustine of Hippo, c. 397-400 AD

From North Africa's greatest Latin writer

Introduction

Augustine of Hippo (354-430 AD) was born in Thagaste (modern Souk Ahras, Algeria) and became bishop of Hippo Regius (modern Annaba, Algeria). His Confessions, written around 397-400 AD, is one of the masterpieces of Western literature—the first true autobiography, addressed directly to God.

This famous passage from Book X, known by its opening words Sero te amavi ("Late have I loved you"), expresses Augustine's regret at having sought God in external things rather than within himself. The rhetorical structure—with its antitheses, parallelisms, and sensory imagery—has been compared to psalmody.

This text is uniquely appropriate for Kartadzinu: Augustine was North African, writing in the very region and era where African Romance would have been spoken. His Latin represents the educated register of Roman Africa.

This translation showcases:

  • Vocative constructions: Direct address to the divine
  • Antithesis and parallelism: Preserving Augustine's rhetorical structure
  • Sensory vocabulary: Sight, smell, taste, touch
  • Construct state: For intimate possession (pats-tea "your peace")
  • Betacism: The b/v merger characteristic of African Latin

The Text / Lu Tekstu

Latin (Original)

Sero te amavi,
pulchritudo tam antiqua et tam nova,
sero te amavi!

Et ecce intus eras et ego foris,
et ibi te quaerebam,
et in ista formosa, quae fecisti,
deformis inruebam.

Mecum eras, et tecum non eram.

Ea me tenebant longe a te,
quae si in te non essent, non essent.

Vocasti et clamasti
et rupisti surditatem meam.

Coruscasti, splenduisti
et fugasti caecitatem meam.

Fragrasti, et duxi spiritum,
et anhelo tibi.

Gustavi et esurio et sitio.

Tetigisti me,
et exarsi in pacem tuam.

Tatsasti me,
e ardai en pats-tea.

English

Late have I loved you,
beauty so ancient and so new,
late have I loved you!

And behold, you were within and I was outside,
and there I sought you,
and into these beautiful things you made
I rushed, misshapen.

You were with me, but I was not with you.

They held me far from you—
those things which, if they were not in you, would not be.

You called and cried out
and shattered my deafness.

You flashed, you shone,
and you scattered my blindness.

You breathed fragrance, and I drew in breath,
and I pant for you.

I tasted, and I hunger and thirst.

You touched me,
and I burned for your peace.

Grammatical Features

Betacism

Note how Latin vocasti becomes bokasti—the b/v merger characteristic of African Latin, which Kartadzinu preserves.

Construct State for Abstract Possession

Augustine's intimate address to God calls for the construct state in Kartadzinu:

  • surditate-mea "my deafness" — not *la surditate mea
  • tsekitate-mea "my blindness" — part of the speaker's being
  • pats-tea "your peace" — intimate divine attribute

Imperfective vs. Perfective

The passage contrasts Augustine's former state (imperfective) with God's decisive actions (perfective):

  • kerkaba, rusaba (imperfective) — ongoing, incomplete seeking
  • bokasti, klamasti, rumppisti (perfective) — completed divine actions

The Five Senses

Augustine's famous sensory sequence is preserved:

  • Hearing: bokasti e klamasti — you called and cried
  • Sight: koruskasti, splendisti — you flashed, you shone
  • Smell: fragrasti — you breathed fragrance
  • Taste: gustai — I tasted
  • Touch: tatsasti me — you touched me

Historical Note

Augustine wrote in the literary Latin of educated North Africa. While the Confessions represents a high register, the underlying phonology of his region—including betacism and the preservation of velars—would have influenced his pronunciation. Kartadzinu imagines how this passage might sound in the vernacular Romance that developed alongside Classical Latin in Roman Africa.
