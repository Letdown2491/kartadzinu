Semen Est Sanguis / Semente Es Lu Sangu

Apologeticum L (Chapter 50) — Tertullian of Carthage, 197 AD

The first great Latin Christian writer

Introduction

Quintus Septimius Florens Tertullianus (c. 155-220 AD) was born in Carthage and became the first major Christian author to write in Latin. His fiery rhetoric and legal training produced some of the most memorable phrases in Christian literature. He coined terms still used today: trinitas (Trinity), sacramentum (sacrament), and persona (person, in the theological sense).

The Apologeticum was written in 197 AD during the reign of Septimius Severus, when Christians faced sporadic persecution. Addressed to Roman provincial governors, it is a brilliant legal defense of Christianity—and an attack on pagan hypocrisy.

This passage from Chapter 50 contains Tertullian's most famous words: "Semen est sanguis Christianorum"—"The blood of Christians is seed." Often misquoted as "the blood of martyrs is the seed of the Church," the original is more striking: persecution doesn't destroy Christianity but plants it.

This text is uniquely appropriate for Kartadzinu: Tertullian was Carthaginian, writing in the city whose name our language bears. His Latin—passionate, epigrammatic, and African—set the tone for all subsequent North African Christian literature.

This translation showcases:

  • Rhetorical parallelism: Balanced clauses and antithesis
  • Imperative and hortatory: Direct address to persecutors
  • Agricultural metaphor: Sowing, reaping, seed
  • Betacism: The b/v merger (biktoria, bensere)
  • VSO for emphasis: Dramatic word order

The Text / Lu Tekstu

Latin (Original)

Nec quicquam tamen proficit
exquisitior quaeque crudelitas vestra:
illecebra est magis sectae.

Plures efficimur
quotiens metimur a vobis:
semen est sanguis Christianorum.

Multi apud vos
ad tolerantiam doloris et mortis hortantur,
ut Cicero in Tusculanis,
ut Seneca in Fortuitis,
ut Diogenes, ut Pyrrhon, ut Callinicus.

Nec tamen tantos inveniunt verba discipulos
quantos Christiani factis docendo.

Illa ipsa obstinatio quam exprobratis
magistra est.

Quis enim non contemplatione eius
concutitur ad requirendum
quid intus in re sit?

Quis non, ubi requisivit, accedit?
Ubi accessit, pati exoptat?

Inde est quod ibidem
sententiis vestris gratias agimus.

Ut est aemulatio divinae rei et humanae:
cum damnamur a vobis,
a Deo absolvimur.

Kartadzinu

E niun koza profita
kwalkumke krudelitate bosta plu ekskisita:
es plu tosta un iletsebra de la sekta.

Plu numerosus debenimu
kada bes ke simu mesuratus de bos:
semente es lu sangu de lus Kristjanus.

Multus apud bos
eksortantur a tolerare doloru e morte,
kom Tsitsero en las Tuskulanas,
kom Seneka en lus Fortuitus,
kom Diogenes, kom Pirrone, kom Kalliniku.

Ma non troban las parabolas tantu diskipulus
kwantu lus Kristjanus insenjantes kon fatsos.

Kela ipsa obstinatsione ke bos reprobates
es magistra.

Ke omu enimpse, kontemplandula,
non es konkussu a kerkare
ke koza es intus en la re?

Ki, avendu kerkatu, non atsede?
Ki, avendu atsedutu, non desira patire?

De iki es ke, en lu logu medesmu,
agemu gratsias a sententsjas-bostas.

Sik es la emulatsione de kozas dibinas e humanas:
kandu simu kondemnatus de bos,
simu absolvutus de Deu.

English

Yet nothing profits
whatever cruelty of yours is more exquisite:
it is rather an enticement to the sect.

We become more numerous
every time we are mowed down by you:
the blood of Christians is seed.

Many among you
exhort to the endurance of pain and death—
Cicero in the Tusculans,
Seneca on Chance,
Diogenes, Pyrrho, Callinicus.

Yet words do not find as many disciples
as Christians teaching by their deeds.

That very obstinacy you reproach
is our teacher.

For who, contemplating it,
is not shaken to inquire
what lies within the matter?

Who, having inquired, does not draw near?
Who, having drawn near, does not desire to suffer?

Hence it is that, in that very place,
we give thanks for your sentences.

Such is the rivalry of divine and human things:
when we are condemned by you,
we are absolved by God.

Grammatical Features

Betacism

The characteristic African Latin b/v merger appears throughout:

  • bosta/bos < Latin vestra/vos "your/you"
  • debenimu < Latin devenimus "we become"
  • abendu < Latin habendo "having"

Passive Constructions

Tertullian's legal rhetoric uses many passives, rendered in Kartadzinu with the passive participle + essere:

  • simu mesuratus "we are mowed down"
  • simu kondemnatus "we are condemned"
  • simu absolvutus "we are absolved"

Rhetorical Questions

Tertullian's prosecutorial style uses rapid rhetorical questions:

  • Ke omu... non es konkussu? "What person is not shaken?"
  • Ki... non atsede? "Who does not draw near?"
  • Ki... non desira patire? "Who does not desire to suffer?"

The Gerund/Gerundive

Tertullian's compressed style uses gerunds and gerundives extensively:

  • kontemplandula "contemplating it" (gerund with object)
  • abendu kerkatu "having inquired" (perfect gerund)
  • insenjantes "teaching" (present participle)

The Famous Phrase

The climactic sentence "Semen est sanguis Christianorum" becomes "Semente es lu sangu de lus Kristjanus" in Kartadzinu. The metaphor is agricultural: when you harvest (mow down) wheat, seeds scatter and produce more wheat. So too with Christians—persecution spreads the faith.

Historical Note

Tertullian wrote during a period of intermittent persecution. The Apologeticum was addressed to provincial governors, using the legal and rhetorical conventions they would recognize. His African Latin style—epigrammatic, paradoxical, and fierce—influenced all later North African Christian writers, including Cyprian and Augustine.

The passage showcases Tertullian's characteristic technique: turning Roman accusations back on the accusers. They call Christians "obstinate"—but that obstinacy attracts converts. They condemn Christians—but God acquits them. Every weapon becomes a boomerang.
