Fabulas de Esopu / Aesop's Fables

Traditional Greek fables — c. 6th century BCE

Moral tales with animal characters

Introduction

Aesop's Fables are among the oldest and most widely translated stories in Western literature. Attributed to Aesop, a Greek slave who lived around 620-564 BCE, these brief moral tales use animal characters to illustrate human virtues and vices.

The fables spread throughout the ancient Mediterranean world, translated into Latin by Phaedrus (1st century CE) and later Avianus (4th-5th century CE). North African readers would have known these Latin versions, which were used in schools to teach both language and ethics.

These three fables showcase different aspects of Kartadzinu:

  • The Tortoise and the Hare: Narrative past tenses, dialogue, comparative constructions
  • The Crow and the Pitcher: Problem-solving sequence, telicity markers, instrumental constructions
  • The Ant and the Grasshopper: Seasonal vocabulary, imperfective vs perfective contrast, conditional sentences

I. La Testudine e lu Lepore

The Tortoise and the Hare

Moral: Slow and steady wins the race

English

A Hare once mocked a Tortoise for being so slow.

"Do you ever get anywhere?" he asked with a laugh.

"Yes," replied the Tortoise, "and I get there sooner than you think. Let us run a race."

The Hare, believing the race to be a joke, agreed. The Fox was chosen as judge, and he marked the course and started them off.

The Hare darted ahead, and soon was so far in front that he decided to take a nap. "I can easily catch up to that slowpoke," he thought.

Meanwhile, the Tortoise kept walking, slowly but steadily, never stopping.

When the Hare awoke, he saw the Tortoise near the finish line. He ran as fast as he could, but it was too late. The Tortoise had won.

Slow and steady wins the race.

Kartadzinu

Un Lepore una ves mokau una Testudine porke ela era tan lenta.

"Arrivas tu umkuam a alkun loku?" demandau ridendo.

"Si," respondau la Testudine, "e arrivu plu tsedu ke tu pensas. Korramu una kursa."

Lu Lepore, kredendo ke la kursa era un goku, akonsentistu. Lu Bolpe fue elektu kome djuditsi, e elu markau lu kursu e partiveru.

Lu Lepore kursau avante, e tostu era tan longe en fronte ke dekidau dormire. "Possu fasilmente atrapare kela lenta," pensau.

Entretantu, la Testudine kontinuava kaminando, lentamente ma konstantemente, numkuam parando.

Kuandu lu Lepore sveglausu, vidau la Testudine kirkum la linea finale. Kursau tan rapidu kome podeva, ma era trop tard. La Testudine aviva vintu.

Lentu e konstante vinse la kursa.

Grammatical Features

  • Perfective narrative: mokau, demandau, respondau, akonsentistu — completed actions driving the plot
  • Imperfective background: kontinuava kaminando — ongoing action while other events occur
  • Comparative: plu tsedu ke tu pensas "sooner than you think"
  • Gerund: ridendo, kaminando, parando — manner expressions

II. Lu Korbu e la Urna

The Crow and the Pitcher

Moral: Necessity is the mother of invention

English

A Crow, half-dead with thirst, came upon a pitcher that had once been full of water. But when he put his beak into the mouth of the pitcher, he found that only a little water was left, and he could not reach it.

He tried and tried, but at last had to give up in despair.

Then a thought came to him. He picked up a pebble and dropped it into the pitcher. Then he took another pebble and dropped it in. Then another. Then another.

Little by little, the water rose until at last he could reach it. And so he saved his life.

Necessity is the mother of invention.

Kartadzinu

Un Korbu, mesu-mortu de site, arrivau a una urna ke era statu plena de akua. Ma kuandu mittau beku-seu en la buka de la urna, trovau ke solu un poku de akua restava, e non podeva atindjerla.

Tentau e tentau, ma a la fine devau abandonare en desperatsione.

Alora una idea venau a elu. Prendau un kalkulu e getau-lu en la urna. Alora prendau un altru kalkulu e getau-lu. Alora un altru. Alora un altru.

Poku a poku, la akua alzausu finkuande a la fine podeva atindjerla. E sifo salvau vita-sua.

Netsesitate es la matre de la inventsione.

Grammatical Features

  • Construct state: beku-seu, vita-sua — body parts and life as inalienable possession
  • Pluperfect: era statu plena "had been full" — prior state
  • Reflexive: alzausu "rose (itself)," salvau "saved"
  • Repetitive action: Alora un altru. Alora un altru. — stylistic repetition
  • Telicity: finkuande marking the endpoint of the rising water

III. La Formika e lu Grilu

The Ant and the Grasshopper

Moral: It is best to prepare for the days of necessity

English

All summer long, the Grasshopper sang and danced and played, while the Ant worked hard gathering food for the winter.

"Why do you work so hard?" asked the Grasshopper. "Come and sing with me!"

"I am storing food for winter," said the Ant. "You should do the same."

"Winter is far away," laughed the Grasshopper. "There is plenty of time."

But soon the cold days came. The fields were bare, and snow covered the ground. The Grasshopper had no food and was dying of hunger.

He went to the Ant and begged for something to eat.

"What did you do all summer?" asked the Ant.

"I sang and danced," said the Grasshopper.

"Then dance through the winter," said the Ant, and closed her door.

It is best to prepare for the days of necessity.

Kartadzinu

Totu lu istiu, lu Grilu kantava e saliva e djokava, dume la Formika laborava dure kollegendo nutrimento por lu invernu.

"Porke laboras tan dure?" demandau lu Grilu. "Veni e kanta kon me!"

"Sto amasando nutrimento por lu invernu," disse la Formika. "Tu deves fare lu mesu."

"Lu invernu es longe," ridau lu Grilu. "E abunda tempu."

Ma tostu lus djornus frigidus arrivaru. Lus kampus eran nudus, e la nive kobriva la terra. Lu Grilu non aviva nutrimento e moriva de fame.

Ivau a la Formika e pregau por alko a mandjare.

"Ke fasisti totu lu istiu?" demandau la Formika.

"Kantai e salivi," disse lu Grilu.

"Alora sali totu lu invernu," disse la Formika, e klaudau porta-sua.

Es megljore preparare por lus djornus de netsesitate.

Grammatical Features

  • Imperfective past: kantava, saliva, djokava, laborava — habitual actions throughout summer
  • Perfective past: arrivaru, demandau, disse — completed narrative events
  • Progressive: sto amasando "I am (in the process of) storing"
  • Construct state: porta-sua "her door"
  • Imperative: Veni e kanta! "Come and sing!"
  • Seasonal vocabulary: istiu, invernu, nive, frigidus

Aspectual Contrast in Narrative

The fables demonstrate the core aspectual distinction in Kartadzinu:

  • Imperfective (-ava/-iva): Background, ongoing, or habitual actions
    • La Testudine kontinuava kaminando — "The Tortoise kept walking"
    • Lu Grilu kantava e saliva — "The Grasshopper sang and danced" (habitually)
  • Perfective (-au/-aru): Completed, bounded events
    • Lu Lepore sveglausu — "The Hare woke up"
    • Prendau un kalkulu e getau-lu — "He picked up a pebble and dropped it"

Moral Sayings

The morals use the gnomic present, expressing timeless truths:

  • Lentu e konstante vinse la kursa. — "Slow and steady wins the race."
  • Netsesitate es la matre de la inventsione. — "Necessity is the mother of invention."
  • Es megljore preparare por lus djornus de netsesitate. — "It is best to prepare for days of need."
