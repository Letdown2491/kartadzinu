Super Flumina Babylonis / Supra Lus Fluminus de Babilone

Psalm 137 (Vulgate 136) — c. 6th century BCE

By the rivers of Babylon

Introduction

Psalm 137 is one of the most poignant poems in the Hebrew Bible—a lament of the Jewish exiles in Babylon after the destruction of Jerusalem in 586 BCE. The opening verses, known in Latin by their incipit Super flumina Babylonis ("By the rivers of Babylon"), have inspired countless musical settings from Palestrina to Boney M.

The psalm's themes of exile, memory, and lost homeland resonate powerfully with Kartadzinu. Just as the exiles wept for Jerusalem beside Babylon's rivers, so too might speakers of African Romance have mourned their tongue as Arabic and Berber displaced it across North Africa. The psalm asks: How can we sing our songs in a foreign land?

The Latin Vulgate translation was made by Jerome around 405 AD—roughly contemporary with Augustine and the flowering of North African Christianity. This version shaped Western liturgy for over a millennium.

This translation presents verses 1-6, the lament proper, which forms a complete poetic unit. (Verses 7-9 contain imprecations against Babylon that were removed from Catholic liturgy after Vatican II.)

This translation showcases:

  • Place names: Babilone, Sione, Gjerusalem
  • Imperfective past: Ongoing actions of sitting, weeping, remembering
  • Subjunctive: Conditional and optative constructions
  • Construct state: For body parts and intimate possession
  • Betacism: The b/v merger (Babilone, kaptibus)

The Text / Lu Tekstu

Latin (Vulgate)

1 Super flumina Babylonis,
ibi sedimus et flevimus,
cum recordaremur Sion.

2 Super salices in medio eius
suspendimus citharas nostras.

3 Quoniam ibi interrogaverunt nos
qui captivos duxerunt nos
verba carminis,
et qui adfligebant nos laeti:
"Canite nobis de canticis Sion."

4 Quomodo cantabimus
canticum Domini
in terra aliena?

5 Si oblitus fuero tui, Hierusalem,
in oblivione sit dextera mea.

6 Adhereat lingua mea gutturi meo,
si non recordatus fuero tui,
si non praeposuero Hierusalem
in principio laetitiae meae.

ili sedivemu e ploravemu,
kandu rekordavemu Sione.

2 Supra lus salitses en mediu-ela
suspendivemu kitaras-nostas.

kelus ke kaptibus nos dukserun
parabolas de kantsone,
e kelus ke nos afligebant, laetus:
"Kantate a nos de lus kantikus de Sione."

4 Komodu kantaremu
lu kantiku du Dominu
en terra aljéna?

5 Se te obliderai, o Gjerusalem,
en oblidione sia destra-mea.

6 Ke adheria lingua-mea a guturu-meu,
se non te rekordaverai,
se non preposiverai Gjerusalem
en lu printsipiu de letitsja-mea.

English

1 By the rivers of Babylon,
there we sat and wept,
when we remembered Zion.

2 Upon the willows in its midst
we hung our harps.

3 For there our captors asked of us
words of song,
and our tormentors, mirth:
"Sing us one of the songs of Zion."

4 How shall we sing
the Lord's song
in a foreign land?

5 If I forget you, O Jerusalem,
let my right hand be forgotten.

6 Let my tongue cling to my palate,
if I do not remember you,
if I do not set Jerusalem
above my highest joy.

Grammatical Features

Imperfective Past for Continuous Action

The psalm's opening depicts ongoing, continuous states of exile—perfect for Kartadzinu's imperfective aspect:

  • sedivemu "we were sitting" (not a single act but a prolonged state)
  • ploravemu "we were weeping" (continuous lamentation)
  • rekordavemu "we were remembering" (ongoing memory)

Construct State for Body Parts

Body parts, as inalienable possessions, take the construct state:

  • destra-mea "my right hand"
  • lingua-mea "my tongue"
  • guturu-meu "my throat/palate"

Subjunctive for Wishes and Conditions

The self-imprecations of verses 5-6 use subjunctive forms:

  • Se te obliderai "If I forget you" (future subjunctive in conditional)
  • sia "let it be" (present subjunctive, optative)
  • Ke adheria "Let it cling" (jussive subjunctive)

Betacism

The characteristic African Latin b/v merger:

  • Babilone (not *Vabilone, but Latin already had B)
  • kaptibus < Latin captivos "captives"
  • sedivemu < Latin sedebamus (intervocalic b preserved)

Thematic Resonance

For Kartadzinu, this psalm carries a special poignancy. The exiles' question—"Komodu kantaremu lu kantiku du Dominu en terra aljena?"—echoes across centuries. How do we sing in a foreign land? How do we preserve our songs, our language, our identity when displaced?

African Romance, like the songs of Zion, fell silent. But unlike the Jewish exiles who returned and rebuilt, the speakers of Kartadzinu's ancestor languages never recovered their tongue. The Arabic conquest transformed North Africa permanently. This psalm, then, is both a historical document and a meditation on linguistic loss—on what it means when a people can no longer sing their songs.

Historical Note

Jerome translated the Psalms multiple times. The version in the Vulgate comes from his translation of the Greek Septuagint (Psalterium Gallicanum), which became standard in Western Christianity. North African Christians like Augustine would have known this Latin text intimately—it was sung in churches from Carthage to Hippo.
