The Passion of Perpetua / Pasione de Perpetua

Selections from the Diary — Carthage, 203 AD

One of the earliest writings by a Christian woman

Introduction

Vibia Perpetua was a 22-year-old noblewoman of Carthage, a new mother, and a Christian catechumen. In 203 AD, she was arrested along with other Christians including her slave Felicitas. While awaiting execution in the arena, Perpetua kept a diary—one of the earliest surviving texts written by a Christian woman.

Her account is remarkable for its vivid first-person narrative, powerful visions, and unflinching courage. The text survives in both Latin and Greek, and was so beloved that Augustine of Hippo later preached sermons on it.

This text is uniquely appropriate for Kartadzinu: Perpetua was Carthaginian, writing in the very city that gave African Romance its name. Her Latin represents the educated speech of Roman North Africa two centuries before Augustine.

This translation showcases:

  • First-person narrative: Perpetua's direct, personal voice
  • Vivid imagery: The ladder, the dragon, the combat
  • Family vocabulary: Father, son, brother — using construct state
  • Dialogue: Exchanges with her father and the authorities
  • Perfective aspect: For completed actions in narrative

I. The Vessel / Lu Basu

Perpetua argues with her father about her identity as a Christian.

English

"Father," I said, "do you see this vessel lying here—a pitcher, or whatever it may be?"

And he said, "I see it."

And I said to him, "Can it be called by any other name than what it is?"

And he said, "No."

"So too I cannot call myself anything other than what I am: a Christian."

Then my father, enraged by the word "Christian," threw himself upon me as if to tear out my eyes. But he only shook me, and he departed, vanquished along with his devil's arguments.

Kartadzinu

"Patre," dissai, "bides tu istu basu dzatsente iki—un urkeu, o kekunke ela sia?"

E elu disse, "Lu biju."

E dissai a elu, "Pode essere klamatu per altru nóme ke kelu ke ela e?"

E elu disse, "Non."

"Alsi eu non podu klamare me-mesa altra koza ke kelu ke so: una Kristjana."

Alora patre-meu, enfurjatu per la parabula "Kristjana," rusau su me kom por arankare okulus-meus. Ma solamente me skutau, e partiau, binkitu insimul kon argumentus-sus djabolikus.

II. The Ladder Vision / La Bisione de la Skala

Perpetua's first vision, showing her the path to heaven guarded by a dragon.

English

I saw a ladder of bronze, marvelously great, reaching up to heaven, and so narrow that only one person could climb it at a time.

And on the sides of the ladder were fixed all manner of iron weapons. There were swords, spears, hooks, and knives, so that anyone who climbed carelessly would be torn and their flesh caught on the iron.

And beneath the ladder lay a dragon of enormous size, who lay in wait for those who would ascend, making them afraid to climb.

Saturus went up first—he who later gave himself up freely for our sake, because he had built us up in faith. And when he reached the top of the ladder, he turned and said to me:

"Perpetua, I await you. But take care that the dragon does not bite you."

And I said, "In the name of Jesus Christ, he will not harm me."

And from beneath the ladder, as if afraid of me, the dragon slowly put forth his head. And as though I were treading on the first step, I trod upon his head, and I went up.

Kartadzinu

Bidai una skala de brunzu, mirabilmente granda, atingente al kelu, e tan streta ke solamente una persona podiba askendere-la a una bolta.

E en lus ladus de la skala furu fiksatus tótu tipu de armas de ferru. Furu ili spadas, lantsas, ankus, e kultellus, median ke kekunke ke askendesse sen kura fusesse latsaratu e karne-sea kaptata su lu ferru.

E sub la skala dzatsiba un drakone de grandesa enorma, ke dzatsiba en aspetu de kelus ke askenderessen, fatsendu-lus timoratus de askendere.

Saturu askendau primu—elu ke plu tard se dau liberamente por nós, porke elu nos avivit edifikatu en fede. E kandu elu atingau lu kapu de la skala, tornau e dissit a me:

"Perpetua, te aspetu. Ma kura ke lu drakone non te mordet."

E dissai, "En lu nóme de Jesu Kristu, non me notsera."

E de sub la skala, kom se timoratu de me, lu drakone lentamente ekstendau kapu-seu. E kom se kalkasse lu primu gradu, kalkai su kapu-seu, e askendai.

English

And I saw an immense garden, and in the middle of it a gray-haired man sitting in shepherd's clothing, tall, milking sheep. And standing around him were many thousands dressed in white.

And he raised his head and looked at me and said, "Welcome, child."

And he called me to him and gave me a morsel of the cheese he was making. And I received it with joined hands and ate it. And all those standing around said, "Amen."

And at the sound of that word I awoke, still tasting something sweet.

And immediately I told my brother, and we understood that it would be a passion, and we began to have no hope any longer in this world.

Kartadzinu

E bidai un ortu imensu, e en lu medju de elu un omu de kapillus grisus sedente en bestimentu de pastore, altu, mulgente objas. E stantes kirkum elu furu milus bestitus en blanku.

E elu lebau kapu-seu e regardau me e dissit, "Benbinuta, fila."

E elu me klamau a se e dau a me un morselu de lu kaseu ke elu fatsiba. E retsebai-lu kon manus djuntus e mandzai-lu. E tótus kelus stantes kirkum disseru, "Amen."

E al sóinu de kela parabula ebigilai, aset gustante alkuna koza dulke.

E immediatamente dissai a fratru-meu, e komprendaimu ke fusesse una pasione, e komentsaimu a non abere speransa plu en istu mundu.

III. The Trial / Lu Djuditsu

Perpetua refuses to sacrifice and is condemned to the beasts.

English

When the day of our trial came, we went down to the forum. A great crowd gathered.

We went up to the platform. The others, when questioned, confessed their faith.

Then it came to my turn. And my father appeared there with my son, and drew me down from the step, saying:

"Perform the sacrifice! Have pity on your child!"

And the procurator Hilarianus said, "Spare your father's gray hairs. Spare the infancy of your boy. Offer sacrifice for the welfare of the emperors."

And I answered, "I will not."

Hilarianus asked, "Are you a Christian?"

And I answered, "I am a Christian."

Then he passed sentence on us all and condemned us to the beasts. And we went down cheerfully to the prison.

Kartadzinu

Kandu lu dia de djuditsu-nostru binit, deskendaimu al foru. Una granda multitidine se kongregau.

Askendaimu a la platafórma. Lus altrus, kandu interrogatus, konfessaru fede-sus.

Alora binit turnu-meu. E patre-meu apparau ili kon filu-meu, e tirau me de lu gradu, disente:

"Fa lu sakrifitsju! Ai pietate de infante-te!"

E lu prokuratóre Hilarijanu dissit, "Respeta lus kapillus grisus de patre-te. Respeta la infantsia de filu-te. Ofera sakrifitsju por la salute de lus imperatoris."

E respondai, "Non lu faru."

Hilarijanu demandau, "Ses tu una Kristjana?"

E respondai, "So una Kristjana."

Alora elu passau sententsja su nós tótus e kondemnau-nos a las bestjas. E deskendaimu alegramente a la karsere.

IV. The Combat Vision / La Bisione de Kombatu

The night before her execution, Perpetua dreams of fighting in the arena.

English

The day before we were to fight, I saw in a vision: Pomponius the deacon came to the prison door and knocked loudly. And I went out to him and opened the door for him.

He was dressed in a white robe without a belt, wearing elaborate sandals. And he said to me:

"Perpetua, we await you. Come."

And he took my hand, and we began to walk through rough and winding places. At last, breathless, we arrived at the amphitheater, and he led me into the middle of the arena.

And he said to me, "Do not be afraid. I am here with you, and I will struggle alongside you."

And he departed.

Kartadzinu

Lu dia ante ke pugnaremu, bidai en bisione: Pomponju lu djakonu binit a la porta de la karsere e pulsau fortemente. E eksiai a elu e aperai la porta por elu.

Elu era bestitu en una toga blanka sen tsintura, portante sandaljas elaboratas. E dissit a me:

"Perpetua, te aspektamu. Bene."

E elu prendau manu-mea, e komentsaimu a kaminare per lokus asprus e sinuosus. A la fine, sen flatu, arrivaimu al amfiteatru, e elu me kondussit en lu medju de la arena.

E dissit a me, "Non times. So iki kon te, e luttaru adlatu te."

E partiau.

English

And I looked and saw a huge crowd watching eagerly. And because I knew that I was condemned to the beasts, I wondered why they did not release them against me.

And there came out against me a certain Egyptian, hideous in appearance, with his supporters, to fight with me.

And to me also there came handsome young men as my supporters and helpers. And I was stripped and became a man.

And my supporters began to rub me with oil, as is the custom before a contest. And I saw the Egyptian opposite me rolling in the dust.

Then there came forth a man of marvelous height, so tall that he overtopped the amphitheater. He wore a purple robe with two stripes across the breast, and elaborate sandals made of gold and silver. He carried a rod like a trainer of gladiators, and a green branch with golden apples.

And he called for silence and said:

"If this Egyptian defeats her, he will kill her with the sword. But if she defeats him, she will receive this branch."

And he withdrew.

Kartadzinu

E regardai e bidai una multitidine granda spektante avidamente. E porke sabiba ke era kondemnata a las bestjas, miraba porke non las liberaban kontra me.

E eksiau kontra me un tsertu Egjiptianu, horribile en apparentsa, kon supportatores-seus, por pugna kon me.

E a me tamben bineru djobenes belu kom supportatores-meus e adjutatores. E fui despoljata e debinai un omu.

E supportatores-meus komentsaru a friksjonare me kon oleu, kom e la kostuma ante un kontestu. E bidai lu Egjiptianu oppositu me rullante en lu pulbu.

Alora eksiau un omu de altura mirabile, tan altu ke superaba lu amfiteatru. Elu portaba una toga purpurea kon duas strias trans lu petu, e sandaljas elaboratas faktas de oru e argentu. Elu portaba una birga kom un magistru de gladjatores, e un ramu berde kon pomas aureas.

E elu klamau por silentsju e dissit:

"Se istu Egjiptianu la binke, la oktsidra kon la spada. Ma se ela lu binke, retsebra istu ramu."

E elu se retirau.

English

And we approached each other and began to exchange blows. He tried to grab my feet, but I kept striking his face with my heels.

And I was lifted up into the air, and I began to strike him as though I were not treading on the earth.

But when I saw there was a pause, I joined my hands, interlocking my fingers, and I caught hold of his head. And he fell on his face, and I trod upon his head.

And the crowd began to shout, and my supporters began to sing psalms. And I went up to the trainer and received the branch.

And he kissed me and said, "Daughter, peace be with you."

And I began to walk in triumph toward the Gate of Life.

And I awoke. And I understood that I was not to fight with beasts, but against the devil. But I knew that mine was the victory.

Kartadzinu

E nos approksimaimu e komentsaimu a ekskambjare kolpus. Elu tentau prendere pes-meus, ma eu kontinuaba kolpare rostru-seu kon kalkanius-meus.

E fui lebata en lu aere, e komentsai a kolpare-lu kom se non kalkasse su la terra.

Ma kandu bidai ke era una pausa, djuntai manus-meas, interlatsante ditus-meus, e kaptai kapu-seu. E elu kadau su rostru-seu, e kalkai su kapu-seu.

E la multitidine komentsau a klamare, e supportatores-meus komentsaru a kantare psalmus. E askendai al magistru e retsebai lu ramu.

E elu me basjau e dissit, "Fila, pats sia kon te."

E komentsai a kaminare en triomfu bers la Porta de Bita.

E ebigilai. E komprendai ke non pugnaresse kon bestjas, ma kontra lu djabolu. Ma sabiba ke mea era la biktoria.

Grammatical Features

Construct State for Family

Perpetua's narrative centers on family relationships, making heavy use of the construct state:

  • patre-meu "my father" — appears repeatedly
  • filu-meu "my son" — her nursing infant
  • fratru-meu "my brother" — her confidant
  • kapu-seu "his head" — the dragon's, the Egyptian's

Betacism Throughout

The b/v merger characteristic of African Latin appears consistently:

  • basu (vas), bisione (visio), bita (vita)
  • biktoria (victoria), bene (vene "come")
  • bidai (vidi "I saw"), binit (venit "came")

Perfective Narrative

The diary uses perfective aspect for completed past actions:

  • bidai "I saw" — perfective of bidere
  • dissai "I said" — perfective of dire
  • askendai "I climbed" — perfective of askendere
  • ebigilai "I awoke" — perfective of ebigilare

The Ladder and the Dragon

Perpetua's ladder vision echoes Genesis 28 (Jacob's ladder) but adds distinctly African elements. The dragon beneath the ladder recalls both the serpent of Genesis and the great beasts of the arena. Her act of treading on the dragon's head (kalkai su kapu-seu) fulfills Genesis 3:15.

Historical Note

Perpetua died in the arena of Carthage on March 7, 203 AD, during the persecution under Septimius Severus. Her diary, completed by an anonymous editor, became one of the most beloved texts of early Christianity. Augustine preached multiple sermons on her feast day, and her cult spread throughout the Mediterranean world.

As a Carthaginian noblewoman, Perpetua wrote in the educated Latin of Roman Africa. Her text represents the same linguistic environment that would develop into African Romance—making this translation a kind of homecoming for her words.
